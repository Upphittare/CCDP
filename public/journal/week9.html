Week 09 · 5 May

Trees, prosthetics, premises

on biomimicry and the question we never asked

This week's lecture focused on dismantling the traditional hierarchy of Human Exceptionalism by showing the hidden intelligence of the natural world. We learned about the ‘Wood Wide Web’ trees use to communicate and share resources, the fact that plants can perceive pain, and experiments showing animals have a complex sense of fairness, empathy, and morality. We also talked about biomimicry and prosthetics — like how integrating natural engineering (cheetah-inspired prosthetic legs) with the human body challenges the whole concept of being ‘disabled’, potentially turning it into being super-abled. That flip — from ‘disabled’ to ‘super-abled’ — is honestly another one of those Eiffel Tower / Distant Horizons moments I wrote about in . It's not new information, it's the same situation suddenly seen from a totally different altitude.

Honestly though, while the scientific evidence about plant communication and animal morality was fascinating, my reaction was kind of calm. To me it just isn't that shocking. Plants and animals have always been living beings — realising they have their own survival mechanisms, intelligence, and ways of interacting with the world feels more like a natural truth than some sudden revelation. They're alive, and life inherently looks for connection and balance.

What I want to actually push back on this week isn't anything about plants or animals — it's the academic narrative around humanity's relationship with nature. The lecture kept framing its arguments against the idea that ‘humanity has arrogantly believed itself to be the absolute master of the Earth’. When I see a claim that big, my instinct is to ask first whether the premise is even true, before asking why. (Same instinct I had in with chaos theory — sweeping claims about ‘humanity’ or ‘the universe’ usually deserve a second look before you accept them.)

Is humanity, as a whole, really that arrogant? I don't think the premise holds up. It's true that certain segments of humanity — driven by hyper-industrialisation or extreme capitalism — have acted with reckless arrogance towards nature. But can those specific factions really represent the entire human race? Throughout history, tons of indigenous cultures, agricultural societies, and modern environmentalists have lived with deep reverence for the natural world. Slapping the label of ‘arrogant master’ onto the entire species is an oversimplification. Before we can genuinely learn from nature through biomimicry to fix our modern crises, I think we need to stop using these generalised, guilt-driven labels and look at our actually pretty diverse human histories more honestly.
