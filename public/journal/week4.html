Week 04 · 23 March

Visual magic

on Méliès, AI video, and the collapse of seeing

This week's tutorial took us to the NGV, and looking at the various art pieces there got me thinking back to the lecture about early photography and cinema. When Georges Méliès first used crude editing to create visual magic, he basically opened a Pandora's box for manipulating reality. And now we've hit some weird new peak with AI video generation. I clearly remember just a year or two ago, AI-generated videos — like that absurd clip of The Rock eating rocks — were glitchy and pretty hilarious. This year though, AI videos have become almost flawless. You honestly can't tell them apart from real footage anymore.

That speed of evolution makes me kind of anxious, since it's eventually going to mess with the authenticity of evidence in our judicial systems. Seeing isn't really believing anymore. This is exactly what I was getting at in — the unsettling feeling people have today isn't because reality became fake, it's because the fake stuff got too convincing. AI video might be where that line finally collapses.

From a creative angle though, it's a huge liberation. Ideas that used to only exist in your head can now be made real with one prompt. Kind of like how early audiences reacted to the Lumière brothers' short films — we don't actually know yet what this new medium is going to bring. Tech that seems useless might end up changing the world, and overhyped stocks might crash overnight. We can only take it one step at a time, the same way the early cinema pioneers did.
