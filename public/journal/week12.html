Week 12 · final lecture

Followers, fans, and leftover pizza

on leadership, Bandcamp, remix culture, and what we close out with

The final lecture this semester opened with a critique of our modern obsession with leadership. We're constantly told that everyone has to be a leader, but the professor's point was that a movement is really created by the first few people brave enough to follow. That reframing stuck with me.

I see this a bit differently because I grew up in East Asian culture. The West pushes hard for individual leadership; East Asian cultures historically lean toward the group. Being too eager to lead, or showing off your personal value too much, often gets read as arrogance, and it can quietly make others not want to include you. The downside is that teamwork often goes to extremes — either nobody steps up and the whole thing stalls in awkward silence, or one person who really wants the spotlight grabs all the power and shuts everyone else up. It's gotten better in recent years, but the obsession with being ‘the boss’ is a flawed default basically everywhere.

Then the lecture moved on to ‘hybrid technology’ and the professor's critique of big platforms like Spotify that control everything top-down. His push was for open-source mentalities and direct-to-fan models like Bandcamp. This fits perfectly with what I was already saying back in about AI — the real danger isn't the technology, it's monopoly. A lot of tech giants laugh at open-source creators in public while keeping their own algorithms locked in a vault.

The reason ‘creator utopia’ never quite sticks is that creators don't actually own the means of production in the digital age. The big platforms own the distribution, the discoverability, the way you get paid. Creators are stuck inside someone else's algorithm, and most of the value they generate gets skimmed off by the platform. Direct-to-fan models like Bandcamp and Patreon are basically a slow, spontaneous rebellion against that. By connecting straight to the people who actually care about their work, creators claw back some of the value. So it isn't just a dream — it's a real survival strategy that works, at least for some.

At the end, we got to mashups, sampling, and remixing. I don't think you can call this stuff just a ‘paradise of creativity’ or just ‘meaningless algorithmic garbage’ — both takes are too clean. On the good side, remix culture breaks down the gatekeeping of giant culture companies and lets regular people actually participate in making and reshaping art. On the bad side, when it gets pulled into algorithm-capitalism, it churns out an endless stream of samey, hollow, attention-bait content that genuinely makes the internet worse.

The world isn't black and white, and it isn't a zero-sum game either. When we look at digital labour, remix culture, or the whole tangle of art-and-tech we've been working through all term — it asks for the same thing every week has been asking for: looking from multiple sides, checking the actual facts, and being honest about where you stand. That requires staying alert, keeping your thinking clear, and being willing to keep learning even after the semester ends.
