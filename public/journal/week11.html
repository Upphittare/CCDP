Week 11 · post-war to pop

Warhol, Maya, the void

on capitalist alienation, Eastern minds, and meaning that won't sit still

The lecture this week shifted into the post-World War II era, moving from existentialism all the way to Andy Warhol and Pop Art. Warhol's endless reproduction of car crashes and celebrities felt less like art and more like a grim prophecy of our current media landscape. We are constantly exposed to a flood of information that simply numbs us. I see this as a profound alienation of humanity, very similar to capitalist alienation. The very technologies and media systems we created have turned back to oppress and dominate us. People are no longer treated as human beings, they are reduced to spare parts or commercial products, just packaged in a much more luxurious digital shell today.

To counter this Western industrial cynicism, the professor introduced Eastern philosophies, specifically the concepts of Karma and the illusion of Maya. My thoughts on this are mixed. Buddhism emphasises the immense power of the mind, suggesting that how we perceive the world is entirely dictated by our internal state. While the physical universe operates on strict material causality, the human mind is boundless. In our minds, anything is possible, and sometimes a desired effect can even dictate the cause. This proves that the world we actively engage with is not strictly material at all, simply because everyone's internally constructed reality is entirely different.

This brings us back to Sartre and the existentialist idea that the universe lacks fundamental meaning. The lecture described this emptiness as something that could be bleak but also completely liberating. I can see the shadows of this existential struggle in many people around me today as they try to find meaning in a commodity-driven world. As for myself, I'm still unsure where I stand on it — maybe not having a definitive answer is the most honest place to sit, for now.
