Week 10 · group presentations

Liminal spaces & let's play

on form, content, and the void between rooms

Week 10 was the culmination of our semester: the group presentations. For our project, we chose to explore the uncanny phenomenon of ‘liminal spaces’ — those unsettling, empty transitional spaces that trigger nostalgia and existential dread, specifically linking them to childhood shadows and trauma. Rather than delivering a dry, academic slide deck, we decided to construct a playable liminal environment and present our research through a ‘YouTube Let's Play’ video format.

Initially, our group discussed delivering a traditional live presentation. But applying the adaptive intelligence we discussed in , we realised a live speech would shatter the eerie, atmospheric tension essential to liminal spaces. By producing a pre-recorded, curated video, we could precisely control the pacing, sound design, and visual impact. Presenting our game as a YouTube-style let's-play allowed us to critique modern internet aesthetics using the very media format that birthed them. It was a perfect integration of form and content, and the live presentation time went by incredibly fast.

Sitting in the audience, both during our class and when observing the remaining groups on Monday of Week 11, I was deeply impressed by the diverse visual styles of our peers. During our session, one group particularly caught my attention with their exploration of Frutiger Aero — that early-to-mid 2000s design aesthetic characterised by glossy textures, tech-optimism, and green-blue eco-futurism. It was fascinating to see how they captured tech-nostalgia, contrasting beautifully with our eerie, liminal exploration of the past. Later, on Monday of Week 11, two other presentations stood out: one analysing the virtual architecture and social spaces within Roblox, and another dissecting the transmedia and visual language of Japanese Anime.

Looking back over these weeks of preparation and peer review, I am faced with the ultimate question: has this ‘unrestricted, mixed-bag’ style of artistic and technological education genuinely reshaped my thinking, or is it just creative self-indulgence?

To be honest, I choose not to answer that yet.

It is too early to tell. I don't believe in tidy, forced academic conclusions. The value of this class shouldn't be summarised in a neat paragraph. Just like the unpredictable systems we studied in , the true impact of these ideas will only reveal itself in the future. For now, the presentation is done, and we take it one step at a time.
