Week 01 · 2 March

Breaking the labels

adaptive intelligence in the twenty-first century

Arts
SCIENCES
a wall built in the nineteenth century, still standing in mine

The lecture this week hit me pretty hard, especially the professor's critique of the nineteenth-century specialised education model. It immediately reminded me of the high school system back in my country, where students get rigidly divided into Arts (history, literature) or Sciences (physics, chemistry). Basically a labelling system. Once you pick, you become stereotyped as a STEM guy or an Arts girl, and it breeds these terrible prejudices — like the absurd idea that studying arts makes you some kind of useless decorative vase. The sad part is most students don't even pick based on what they actually like; they pick based on job prospects, or just whatever their parents want. The professor's point about cross-disciplinary thinking made me realise that breaking these walls isn't really about mixing subjects. It's more about helping people with totally different mindsets actually understand each other and look at the world without prejudice.

When he talked about ‘adaptive intelligence’ and shared the story of the hospitalised student who used a taped iPad to do his presentation, I thought that was kind of perfect. To me that's exactly what human curiosity looks like — the pure desire to figure something out. That drive is what's been pushing humans forward for tens of thousands of years. I'll admit I struggled to immediately come up with a cross-disciplinary example like the tree-inspired water pump (biomimicry) he mentioned in class, and honestly I think that hesitation itself says something about how rigidly categorised my own education has been.

The point of education shouldn't be turning us into robots that memorise stuff — robots already do that better than us.

We're living in an era flooded with AI right now. But I think fundamentally, AI is just a probability engine doing predictive word combinations. It doesn't really have creativity, vitality, or imagination that can adapt. The point of education shouldn't be turning us into robots that memorise stuff — robots already do that better than us. It should be about getting back our distinctly human ability to creatively problem-solve, like that student with the iPad did.
